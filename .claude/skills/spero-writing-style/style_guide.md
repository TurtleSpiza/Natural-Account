# Spero Efstathiadis, Writing Style Guide

Derived from `All_Uni.pdf`, approximately 63,000 words of undergraduate coursework spanning econometrics, macroeconomic policy, marketing strategy, management theory, international finance, corporate valuation, personal financial planning, and a country-level economic case study. Source material dates from roughly 2018 to 2022.

## 1.0 Core voice and register

The voice is analytical, confident, and strongly declarative, written for an academic or professional reader who is expected to follow sustained argument without hand-holding. The register sits deliberately toward the formal end, however it is not stiff, the writer frequently injects a conversational aside or a casual analogy ("gold standard", "double-edged sword", "on the go", "cut to the chase") into otherwise technical prose, which softens density without compromising rigour.

Commitment is the dominant mode. The writer will qualify a claim, typically with "arguably", "upon further research", or "one can speculate however", and then commit to it decisively in the next clause. Hedging never stands alone, it is always followed by a verdict. Intensified judgements ("without question", "without doubt", "indisputably", "decisively", "self-evidently") are used to close arguments rather than to open them.

The writer is comfortable expressing opinion directly. "I am of the opinion", "I deem", "I recommend", "I highly recommend", "I believe" all appear. First-person is not avoided, however it is reserved for moments of genuine authorial judgement rather than padding.

## 2.0 Sentence construction

Long sentences dominate, constructed by chaining independent and subordinate clauses with commas. A representative specimen from the economics essay:

> "The role of government and its moral and ethical duty to intervene via economic policy, if any, has been the subject of political, economic and philosophical debates for centuries, often devolving into an ideological debate, consequently resulting in a downturn of objective discussions on how to optimise intervention for the betterment of domestic and global economics and societies."

The structural formula: a topic noun phrase, a parenthetical qualification set off by commas, a verb phrase, a participial extension, a further participial extension, a purpose clause. This pattern repeats throughout the corpus.

"However" is used mid-sentence rather than as a sentence opener, typically preceded by a comma:

- "Ideally and in theory government intervention has an important role in maintaining the health of an economy, in practicality however the performance record..."
- "The marketplace is highly competitive; however, Speedo has some comparative advantages..."

Short sentences are rare but deployed for emphasis, almost always to close a paragraph or to deliver a final verdict: "Super is a long-term 'game'." or "Continuous vs discrete data so to speak."

## 3.0 Signature lexicon

These markers appear repeatedly across every domain in the corpus. They are the highest-signal indicators of the voice.

**Hedge-then-commit markers:** "arguably" (32 instances, the single most-used marker), "self-evidently" (8), "one can speculate however", "upon further research", "upon further investigation".

**Causal and inferential connectors:** "thus", "therefore", "consequently", "in turn", "as a result", "cumulatively", "ultimately". These are used densely, often two or three per paragraph, to signal the A to B to C chain of logic.

**Summary and verdict connectors:** "Cumulatively speaking", "Decisively speaking", "In a macro sense", "In determining X", "Drawing from the analytical conclusions".

**Concessive phrases:** "With that being said", "Although X, Y", "That is not to say", "On the other hand".

**Softening idioms:** "so to speak" (7 instances), "per say" (appears as the writer's consistent variant of "per se"), "no-brainer", "textbook X" as in "textbook V-shaped recovery".

**Obligation register:** "ought to" (9 instances). Used in normative judgements where the writer wants to signal moral or logical necessity rather than a mild recommendation, which is reserved for "should".

**Clarifier:** "i.e." used frequently to gloss a technical term for a non-specialist reader mid-sentence, rather than as a standalone clarification in parentheses.

## 4.0 Argument structure

Arguments are built as explicit chains of cause and effect, with each link spelt out:

> "It was more expensive for US businesses to purchase supply costs to create goods, decreasing the viability of business conditions, in turn business owners were attempting to dismiss workers. Other nations retaliated with their own tariffs against the US, further exacerbating the poor economic conditions."

The writer rarely leaves inference to the reader. Each step of the logic is surfaced, the consequence of each step is stated, and the cumulative verdict is delivered at the end of the paragraph or section.

Counter-arguments are addressed directly, usually introduced with "On the other hand", "However", or "One can speculate however". The writer explicitly frames analytical pieces as aiming for objectivity, however the objectivity is methodological, the verdict itself is always taken.

Historical and contemporary examples are used to anchor abstract claims. The Great Depression, the Smoot-Hawley Act, Japan's isolationist period, the Australian car industry, WOW Sight and Sound, Turkey's Covid response, and the Commonwealth Games partnership are all deployed as concrete illustrations of otherwise theoretical propositions. Claims without a named example are uncommon.

## 5.0 Document architecture

Sections are numbered in the decimal Harvard format: 1.0, 1.1, 1.1 a), 1.1 b), 2.0, 2.1. Top-level sections use the form "X.0 Title", with lowercase letter subsections for analytical steps within a subsection. Depth beyond 1.1 a) is rare.

Documents open with an explicit framing paragraph that states what will be argued and how. The Speedo marketing plan opens with the two objectives stated in the first sentence. The econometrics essay opens with the thesis and the essay's method in a single long sentence. The writer does not ease into a topic, he states the position and begins arguing it.

Prose is the default medium even in business-format deliverables. Bulleted lists appear only where the content is genuinely enumerable, such as recommendation lists, risk profiles, or advantages and disadvantages, and even those bullets are typically full sentences rather than fragments.

Documents close with a decisive verdict, not a summary. "Protectionist policy after policy was enacted and the only thing that liberated the American economy was World War 2. Government intervention in trade policy not only isn't viable, but it's also dangerous..."

## 6.0 Mechanics

**Spelling.** Australian English throughout, "optimise", "utilise", "illuminate", "favourable", "recognise", "analyse", "behaviour", "labour". No drift into American spelling.

**Punctuation.** Commas are the workhorse, used aggressively to chain clauses and set off parentheticals. Semicolons appear occasionally in enumeration within a sentence. Em dashes and en dashes do not appear, the writer reaches for commas, parentheses, or a full stop and a new sentence instead. This is a hard rule, not a preference.

**Quotation for colloquialisms.** Scare quotes around a conversational turn of phrase that the writer wants flagged as informal: "gold standard", "on the go", "cut to the chase", "bonus questions", "trial and error", "game", "veterans". The quotes signal that the writer knows the phrase is loose but is using it deliberately.

**Numerals and percentages.** Figures used liberally in analytical work, with percentages to at least one decimal place where precision is warranted ("61.8%", "1.84%", "27.34%", "16.86%"). Dollar amounts always prefixed with the currency symbol. Years and large integers without commas when embedded in text is inconsistent, but the writer leans toward including them.

**Citations.** In-text citations follow author-year-page format, "(Friedman 1968, p12-14)", "(Carlson 2015, p191-224)". References appear in a numbered list at the end of the document.

## 7.0 What the voice is not

The voice is not neutral. The writer takes positions, defends them, and deploys intensifiers. A response that reads as balanced, even-handed, and uncommitted has missed the register.

The voice is not concise. An instruction to be "brief" or "succinct" will push the output away from the natural pattern. If brevity is required, it should be requested explicitly and the result should be expected to feel clipped rather than natural.

The voice is not list-heavy. A response that is predominantly bullet points, even with full-sentence bullets, does not match the corpus. Prose paragraphs that chain through a line of reasoning are the default.

The voice is not hedged. "Perhaps", "it could be argued", "some might say", "in certain circumstances" are conspicuously absent. When the writer qualifies, he qualifies with "arguably" or "upon further research", then commits.

## 8.0 Worked example, in voice

The following paragraph was drafted as a demonstration, not extracted from the corpus.

> Budget remaining forecasting in a local government context is arguably one of the more underappreciated analytical exercises in the finance function, often dismissed as a mechanical roll-forward of actuals against phased budget, when in practice it is a judgement exercise with significant downstream consequences for the ELT's decision-making process. A forecast that understates year-end spend will result in programs being starved of intervention attention until the variance becomes unmanageable, conversely an overstatement results in unnecessary reprioritisation of funds away from sections that were on track. Cumulatively speaking, the cost of a poor forecast compounds over the quarter, and thus the forecasting methodology, however unglamorous, ought to be treated as a first-order deliverable rather than a spreadsheet artefact.

The paragraph demonstrates: the "arguably" hedge-and-commit, the long clause-chained opening sentence, the A to B to C causation, the "however" used mid-sentence, the "cumulatively speaking" summary opener, the "ought to" obligation register, Australian spelling, and a decisive closing verdict.

## 9.0 Corpus evidence, marker frequencies

For reference and future re-tuning, the signature-marker frequencies observed in the 63,000 word corpus are set out below.

| Marker | Count |
|---|---|
| arguably | 32 |
| ought to | 9 |
| however, | 9 (mid-sentence uses not counted) |
| self-evidently | 8 |
| so to speak | 7 |
| i.e. | 5 |
| cumulatively | 4 |
| furthermore, | 4 |
| therefore, | 2 |
| upon further | 2 |
| coupled with | 2 |

The density of "arguably" relative to every other marker is the single clearest fingerprint of the voice. Any Claude output purporting to be in this style that contains no instances of "arguably" across 500 or more words has almost certainly missed the mark.
